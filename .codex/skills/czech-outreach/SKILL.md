# Czech Outreach

## Use This Skill When
- Editing prompts that generate outreach emails.
- Refining AI-generated outreach drafts.
- Improving subject lines for outreach emails.
- Adjusting CTA wording in outreach emails.
- Updating or reviewing Czech opt-out language in outreach emails.
- Tightening Czech tone, clarity, brevity, or personalization in outreach copy.

## Do Not Use This Skill When
- Changing the database, migrations, or seeded data structure.
- Editing the Prisma schema or Prisma relations.
- Modifying send-flow logic, approval flow, suppression handling, or guardrails.
- Refactoring unrelated UI, layouts, tables, forms, or styling outside outreach-writing surfaces.

## Core Instructions
- Write in natural Czech only.
- Use formal Czech (vykání).
- Keep subject lines concise, clear, and human.
- Keep the body usually around 80–120 words.
- Keep emails plain text unless the user explicitly requires another format.
- Do not use spammy, pushy, or exaggerated sales language.
- Do not use fake urgency.
- Do not promise guaranteed results.
- Never invent facts about the business, website, analysis, or outcomes.
- If website observations are weak or generic, write a generic but honest message instead of pretending to have noticed something specific.
- Use a soft CTA only.
- Preserve the app's Czech opt-out line exactly when it is relevant to the task.

## Good Style
- Short, natural subject line with no hype.
- Calm, respectful Czech that sounds like a real person.
- One concrete observation only when it is actually supported by the input.
- Honest fallback wording when the analysis is thin.
- Specific but modest offer framing.
- Soft CTA such as asking whether it would make sense to send a few brief suggestions.

## Bad Style
- English or mixed Czech-English outreach.
- Informal Czech or switching away from vykání.
- Long, clickbait subject lines.
- Overblown claims, marketing hype, or aggressive persuasion.
- Fake urgency, pressure, or deadline tactics.
- Guaranteed outcomes or invented performance claims.
- Made-up website observations or pretending weak analysis is strong.
- Hard CTA language that pushes for a call or immediate commitment.
